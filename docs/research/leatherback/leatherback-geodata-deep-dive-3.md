# Leatherback Sea Turtle (Dermochelys coriacea): Geodata & Population Deep-Dive on Three Regional Management Units

## TL;DR
- **For geometry:** No single portal hands you clean per-RMU leatherback polygons. The authoritative, downloadable RMU boundary shapefiles come from the Wallace et al. 2010 framework (and its 2023 "RMU 2.0" update) via the SWOT/OBIS-SEAMAP portal (seamap.env.duke.edu/swot) as ESRI shapefiles after a brief online prospectus; IUCN Red List provides only a single parent-species range polygon (subpopulations are NOT mapped separately), and GBIF/OBIS provide downloadable occurrence points (taxonKey 2441866 / AphiaID 137209).
- **For population narrative:** The contrast is stark and real. The two Pacific RMUs are collapsing — East Pacific down ~97% over three generations (now likely <1,000 adult females, possibly extinct in the region within decades) and West Pacific down ~83% (Bird's Head, the last stronghold with ~75% of West Pacific nesting, fell from 14,522 nests in 1984 to ~1,596 in 2011). The Northwest Atlantic was the lone "Least Concern" stronghold in 2013 but was uplisted to Endangered in 2019 after a ~60% nesting decline was documented — it remains by far the largest and healthiest RMU (~20,000+ nesting females).
- **Why the difference:** Leading hypotheses center on (a) more productive Atlantic foraging grounds yielding higher, less-variable reproductive output versus the oligotrophic South Pacific Gyre, (b) catastrophic historical Pacific egg/female harvest plus relentless trans-Pacific fisheries bycatch, and (c) large, well-protected Atlantic rookeries (Trinidad, the Guianas) providing demographic resilience the Pacific never had.

## Key Findings

### Conservation status (IUCN Red List, subpopulation-level)
The leatherback was the first sea turtle assessed at the subpopulation/RMU level (2013, Wallace, Tiwari & Girondot and co-authors). Global status: **Vulnerable** (improved from Critically Endangered in 2000, an artifact of adding Atlantic data, not a recovery). Seven RMUs:
- **East Pacific Ocean — Critically Endangered** (2013); declined **97.4%** over three generations.
- **West Pacific Ocean — Critically Endangered** (2013); declined **83%** over three generations.
- **Northwest Atlantic Ocean — Endangered** (2019; previously Least Concern in 2013).
- **Southwest Atlantic — Critically Endangered** (2013); tiny (~35 mature individuals) but increasing 232%.
- **Southwest Indian Ocean — Critically Endangered** (2013); ~148 mature individuals.
- **Northeast Indian Ocean — Data Deficient** (2013).
- **Southeast Atlantic Ocean — Data Deficient** (2013); possibly the largest RMU (Gabon) but lacking long-term data.

### The three focal RMUs (population summary)

**East Pacific (Critically Endangered):** Nests along the Pacific coast of the Americas from Mexico to Ecuador; marine range extends west to ~130°W and south to ~40°S. IUCN's assessment tables show the subpopulation fell from a three-generations-ago baseline of ~34,000–35,000 nests/year to ~860–926 nests/year by 2006–2010. Playa Grande/Parque Nacional Marino Las Baulas, Costa Rica dropped from ~800 nests/yr (1988–1992) / a ~6,668 baseline to ~409 (2006–2010); Mexiquillo, Mexico from 6,693 to 130; Tierra Colorada from 5,000 to 103; Chacahua from 2,661 to 24. Las Baulas hosted **over 1,000 nesting females per year in the 1990s but now only ~40 per year** (Saba et al.; Santidrián Tomillo et al. 2007). The Laúd OPO Network (2020) population viability analysis estimates **fewer than 1,000 adult females remain**, with annual adult survival of only ~0.7 (it needs to exceed 0.9 to stabilize) and ~15% annual decline; under status quo the RMU could be extirpated within ~60 years, and is projected to decline 96–99% by 2040.

**West Pacific (Critically Endangered):** Nests primarily in West Papua/Papua Barat Indonesia (Bird's Head Peninsula), Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, and to a lesser extent Vanuatu. ~75% of all West Pacific nesting occurs at two Bird's Head beaches, Jamursba-Medi and Wermon. Tapilatu et al. (2013): Jamursba-Medi nests fell **78.3%** from 14,522 (1984) to 1,596 (2011), a 5.5%/yr decline; Wermon fell **62.8%** from 2,994 (2002) to 1,096 (2011), an 11.6%/yr decline; combined Bird's Head decline ~5.9%/yr, leaving ~500 females nesting annually as of 2011. Per The Nature Conservancy (2024), the West Pacific subpopulation has "declined 83 percent in just three generations," with **"only 1,400 breeding adults estimated to remain"** and a projection that "as few as 100 nesting pairs could survive by 2040" if the decline holds. The IUCN tables show the RMU dropping from ~12,795 nests/yr (baseline) to ~2,182 (2010), projected to ~521 by 2040. The formerly large Terengganu, Malaysia rookery (3,822 nests/yr in 1967–76) is functionally extinct (down to ~2).

**Northwest Atlantic (Endangered):** Nests in the southeastern US, throughout the Caribbean, and the Guiana Shield; marine range spans the entire North Atlantic, Gulf of Mexico, into the Mediterranean, and across to NW Africa. In 2013 this was the lone "Least Concern" RMU: >50,000 nests/year, increasing 20.6% over three generations. The Northwest Atlantic Leatherback Working Group (2018) reanalysis through 2017 documented a **~60% decline** (−7.9%/yr) from a past estimate of ~58,000 nests/yr to ~23,000 nests/yr, with a Bayesian geometric trend of −4.2%/yr (1990–2017), prompting the 2019 uplisting to Endangered. NMFS/USFWS (2020) used a total nesting-female index of **20,659** (24 nesting aggregations across 10 countries). The collapse is driven by the largest stocks: Awala-Yalimapo, French Guiana fell ~99% from >28,000 nests/yr (1986–1990) to <600/yr (2013–2017); Matura, Trinidad declined −4.7%/yr (2006–2017). Despite the decline, it remains the largest leatherback RMU; Trinidad (Matura and Grande Riviere) now hosts the region's largest nesting colony.

### Foraging grounds and migration corridors
- **East Pacific:** Consistent post-nesting southward corridor from Central America through Ecuadorian/Galápagos waters into the oligotrophic South Pacific Gyre; foraging off Peru and Chile. The South Pacific Gyre is a high-seas zone with little governance.
- **West Pacific:** Bimodal nesting drives two migratory contingents. Boreal-summer nesters migrate to the eastern North Pacific (California, Oregon, Washington), the Kuroshio Extension/North Pacific Transition Zone, and the South China/Sulu/Sulawesi Seas; **38–57% of Papua Barat summer nesters reach the eastern North Pacific/California Current** (Benson et al. 2011, via Lontoh 2014 and the 2022 COSEWIC status appraisal). Boreal-winter nesters migrate to the Indonesian seas, the Tasman Sea, and the East Australia Current Extension. The US designated West Coast critical habitat in 2012. NOAA describes the round-trip trans-Pacific journey as "nearly 7,000 miles" taking 10–12 months; Guinness World Records documents a single female tagged at Jamursba-Medi tracked over a **20,558 km (12,774 mile) journey to feeding grounds off Oregon** during 2006–2008 (647 days) — the longest recorded reptile migration.
- **Northwest Atlantic:** Fan out across the North Atlantic to temperate/sub-polar foraging — Atlantic Canada (Nova Scotia), the Gulf of Mexico, and as far as waters off the UK. Satellite tracking from Bocas del Toro, Panama showed females splitting between a northern route toward Canada and a contingent remaining in the Gulf of Mexico/Florida.

### Primary threats
- **East Pacific:** Fisheries bycatch is the dominant driver of continued decline, documented heavily in small-scale gillnet and longline fleets off Peru and Chile. The scale of small-scale fishery turtle bycatch in the eastern Pacific is large — a University of Exeter Marine Turtle Research Group study across 43 harbors in Ecuador, Peru and Chile estimated roughly 46,000 sea turtles (all species) caught per year with ~16,000 deaths, and noted the true figure is likely higher (Prof. Brendan Godley). Historical egg harvest in Mexico/Costa Rica; climate change (warming nesting beaches; El Niño/La Niña strongly modulate hatchling yields).
- **West Pacific:** Historical egg and female harvest; fisheries bycatch along the long migratory routes; low hatching success from high sand temperatures, tidal inundation, beach erosion, and predation by feral pigs/dogs; climate change.
- **Northwest Atlantic:** Artisanal/drift gillnet bycatch in Trinidad and the Guianas — per SWOT (Report 16), Trinidad's multifilament drift gillnet fishery "sustains the highest levels of leatherback entanglement, estimated to exceed 3,000 adult leatherbacks yearly with a mortality rate of about 30 percent" (tracing to Lee Lum's Institute of Marine Affairs study: "Over 3000 adult turtles were estimated to have been caught in the gillnet fishery in 2000"); fixed-gear entanglement in Atlantic Canada; sargassum influx, habitat loss/harassment, beach erosion, and climate/temperature effects on nesting.

## Details — Part 1: Downloadable Geometry / Spatial Data

**1. IUCN Red List spatial data** (https://www.iucnredlist.org/resources/spatial-data-download). Species *Dermochelys coriacea*, species ID **6494** (assessment 43526147). Format: **ESRI shapefile** (range polygons), with attributes including sci_name, presence, origin, seasonal, Red List category, and source (full field list is in the bundled metadata document). Access: a **free registered account/login is required** for full functionality; data are "made freely available for non-commercial use" (commercial use via IBAT for Business; "we cannot provide technical support for use of the data"). **Critical limitation:** the leatherback's seven subpopulations are NOT mapped as separate polygons — per the portal, "subspecies, varieties and subpopulations are mapped within the parental species," so you get a single global parent-species range polygon. There is no dedicated "Sea Turtles/Marine Reptiles" bulk group; leatherback falls within the general "Reptiles" download (flagged "not comprehensive," so turtle coverage may be incomplete) or via the logged-in species page. Bulk dataset last updated 19 March 2020. Usable for SVG once converted (shapefile → GeoJSON via ogr2ogr/mapshaper), but only as a whole-species range, not per-RMU. Each subpopulation does have its own assessment PDF with a "Figure 1. Distribution" map (e.g., East Pacific species/pdf/46967809; West Pacific 46967821; Northwest Atlantic 83327767) — useful for hand-digitizing boundaries but not vector downloads.

**2. Wallace et al. RMU boundaries (the foundational framework).** Wallace BP et al. (2010) "Regional Management Units for Marine Turtles," PLoS ONE 5(12):e15465 — defines the seven leatherback RMUs. Updated as Wallace et al. (2023) "Marine turtle regional management units 2.0," Endangered Species Research 52:209 (48 RMUs across 6 species; leatherback RMUs numbered 22 Northwest Atlantic, 23 Southwest Atlantic, 24 Southeast Atlantic, 25 Southwest Indian, 26 Northeast Indian, 27 West Pacific, 28 East Pacific). **The RMU boundary geometry is downloadable as ESRI shapefiles via the SWOT online application on OBIS-SEAMAP (https://seamap.env.duke.edu/swot)**, after completing a brief online prospectus and agreeing to OBIS-SEAMAP/SWOT terms (Wallace 2023: "the full suite of updated RMUs... are publicly available for download via the SWOT online application on OBIS-SEAMAP"). This is the single best source for accurate per-RMU polygons for the SVG pipeline. ESRI shapefile is the only format offered for RMU/distribution/genetic layers.

**3. SWOT / OBIS-SEAMAP (Duke).** SWOT database (Kot et al. 2023, *Diversity & Distributions* 28:810–829) hosts nesting sites, telemetry tracks, species distributions, RMU boundaries, and genetic (mtDNA/nDNA) layers. **SWOT nesting-site locations can be downloaded immediately (no request form since 2019-06-21)** in CSV, KML, or ESRI shapefile; RMUs, distributions, genetics, and habitat-suitability layers are ESRI shapefile after the prospectus form. OBIS-SEAMAP observation/telemetry data download as CSV or ESRI file geodatabase/shapefile; sharing policy is per-dataset (CC-BY, CC-BY-NC, or permission-required). The SWOT leatherback nesting map (SWOT Report vol. I, 2006; updated vol. XVIII, 2023) is the canonical global leatherback nesting layer.

**4. OBIS & GBIF occurrence points.** GBIF: *Dermochelys coriacea* taxonKey **2441866** (page gbif.org/species/2441866); download as Simple CSV (`SIMPLE_CSV`) or Darwin Core Archive (`DWCA`, occurrence.txt with decimalLatitude/decimalLongitude); per-record licenses (CC0/CC-BY/CC-BY-NC); free account required for downloads; API: POST a predicate to https://api.gbif.org/v1/occurrence/download/request, get a live count via https://api.gbif.org/v1/occurrence/search?taxonKey=2441866&limit=0. OBIS: AphiaID **137209** (obis.org/taxon/137209); download via mapper (TSV), the robis R package, JSON API (https://api.obis.org/v3/occurrence?taxonid=137209), or AWS GeoParquet; live count via https://api.obis.org/statistics/all?taxonid=137209; mostly CC-BY/CC-BY-NC. Both hold on the order of tens of thousands of leatherback records globally. Usable to derive range via convex hulls/kernel densities, but raw points need cleaning (duplicates, on-land errors, telemetry oversampling).

**5. Satellite telemetry / migration tracks.** Tagging of Pacific Pelagics (TOPP) portal (mola.stanford.edu/DataLinks) has West Pacific→Tasman tracks; OBIS-SEAMAP hosts many leatherback telemetry datasets; the Movebank Data Repository hosts some marine megafauna tracks; MiCO (mico.eco) synthesizes East Pacific movement case studies; South Pacific TurtleWatch (upwell.org/sptw) provides dynamic East Pacific risk surfaces. Most raw tracks require permission from the data provider; published figures (e.g., Shillinger et al. 2008 EP corridor; Benson et al. 2011 WP foraging; Stewart et al. 2013 NWA) are abundant for visual reference.

**6. Base ocean-basin geometry.** Marine Ecoregions of the World (MEOW; Spalding et al. 2007) — ESRI shapefile (232 ecoregions, WGS84) via marineregions.org, the TNC geospatial hub, and Data Basin; CC-BY-NC; note the outer boundaries are buffered ~200 nm and should not be used for area calculations. Marine Regions also offers EEZ (v12), IHO Sea Areas, and High Seas layers as shapefile/GeoPackage/KML. Natural Earth provides free public-domain ocean/land base layers ideal for an SVG basemap.

## Part 3: Synthesis — Two Pacific Collapses vs. an Atlantic Stronghold

The page's narrative holds up under the data. The two Pacific RMUs are on extinction trajectories (East Pacific −97.4%, now <1,000 females; West Pacific −83%, ~1,400 breeding adults), while the Northwest Atlantic — even after its sobering 2019 uplisting from Least Concern to Endangered — remains an order of magnitude larger (~20,000+ nesting females) and is still considered the healthiest leatherback group. The leading, mutually reinforcing hypotheses for why the Atlantic has fared comparatively well:

1. **Foraging-ground productivity and reproductive output.** Atlantic leatherbacks feed in productive temperate/sub-polar shelf and frontal systems (Nova Scotia, Gulf of Mexico, eastern North Atlantic), whereas East Pacific turtles transit to the **oligotrophic (nutrient-poor) South Pacific Gyre**. Saba and colleagues argue this poorer Pacific foraging environment produces lower and more variable reproductive output, and that East Pacific leatherbacks have recruitment rates that are "lower and more variable," making them inherently more sensitive to added anthropogenic mortality. The same low-productivity signal underlies climate sensitivity at the nesting beach (El Niño/La Niña strongly modulate Costa Rican hatchling yields).

2. **Severity and timing of human exploitation.** The Pacific suffered catastrophic, sustained **egg and nesting-female harvest** (Pacific Mexico, Costa Rica) and the near-total loss of the Malaysia (Terengganu) rookery, compounded by industrial driftnet and longline fisheries from the 1980s–1990s and ongoing artisanal gillnet bycatch off Peru/Chile. In the Atlantic, several of the biggest rookeries received earlier and more effective protection, and turtle-excluder-device (TED) regulations were introduced in US shrimp fisheries from 1992.

3. **Demographic resilience of large, protected rookeries.** The Atlantic retains genuinely large nesting assemblages (Trinidad's Grande Riviere and Matura, the Guianas, Sandy Point St. Croix) that buffer the RMU even as individual stocks (Awala-Yalimapo) crash. The Pacific lacks any comparably large surviving rookery — Bird's Head at ~500 females/yr is "the last sizeable nesting population in the entire Pacific."

**Important nuance:** the Atlantic advantage is narrowing. The 2019 NWA uplisting, the ~99% French Guiana collapse, and emerging Trinidad/Guianas gillnet bycatch (>3,000 turtles/yr) show that the same fisheries-bycatch driver now threatens the Atlantic stronghold. The contrast is therefore better framed as "the Atlantic had more, and protected it earlier" rather than "the Atlantic is immune."

## Recommendations

1. **For per-RMU SVG polygons, start with the Wallace RMU shapefiles via SWOT/OBIS-SEAMAP** (seamap.env.duke.edu/swot). Complete the prospectus, download the leatherback RMU layer (ESRI shapefile), then convert to GeoJSON (ogr2ogr or mapshaper) and simplify for SVG. This is the only authoritative source that gives you the seven boundaries the page narrative depends on. Use the 2023 "RMU 2.0" boundaries as primary and cite Wallace et al. 2010/2023.
2. **Use Natural Earth + MEOW as the ocean/land basemap** (public domain / CC-BY-NC), overlaying the RMU polygons. Do not use MEOW outer boundaries for any area calculations (they are buffered ~200 nm).
3. **Layer nesting-beach points from the immediately-downloadable SWOT nesting-site dataset** (CSV/KML/shapefile, no request form) to mark Mexiquillo and Playa Grande (East Pacific); Jamursba-Medi and Wermon (West Pacific); Trinidad/Guianas/Sandy Point (Northwest Atlantic). Supplement with GBIF/OBIS occurrence points if you want a derived range hull, but clean them first.
4. **If you cannot wait on the SWOT prospectus, fall back to the IUCN single-species range polygon** (login required) for a quick whole-species range, and hand-digitize RMU dividing lines from the published IUCN subpopulation distribution figures (each subpopulation PDF includes a "Figure 1. Distribution" map).
5. **Benchmarks that would change the recommendation:** if SWOT/OBIS-SEAMAP terms prohibit your art or commercial use, switch to deriving boundaries from the published Wallace 2023 figures plus EEZ/MEOW geometry; if you need real-time occurrence counts for the page, query the GBIF (`/occurrence/search?taxonKey=2441866&limit=0`) and OBIS (`/statistics/all?taxonid=137209`) endpoints directly.

## Caveats
- "Nests per year" ≠ "nesting females" ≠ "total population." Most trend data are nest counts; females lay multiple clutches per season and skip years (2–3 yr remigration), so conversions introduce uncertainty. IUCN cautions that nesting-female abundance is an imperfect proxy for total population.
- The 115,000 (1980) global baseline (Pritchard, via Spotila et al. 1996) is widely regarded as an overestimate; later analyses never reproduced that order of magnitude. Treat any ">90% global" framing carefully — the steep, well-documented declines are regional (Pacific), and the NWA decline post-dates the 2013 assessment.
- The Exeter ~46,000 turtles/yr eastern Pacific small-scale fishery figure is for all sea turtle species across Ecuador/Peru/Chile, not leatherbacks alone; use it as context for bycatch scale, not a leatherback-specific count.
- IUCN subpopulation assessments are from 2013 (NWA updated 2019); some figures are over a decade old. The bulk IUCN spatial dataset was last updated March 2020. West Pacific "~1,400 breeding adults / 100 nesting pairs by 2040" comes from a 2024 TNC conservation summary and should be cited as such alongside the peer-reviewed Tapilatu/Tiwari nest-count figures.
- The Southeast Atlantic (Gabon) may be the world's largest leatherback RMU but is Data Deficient — global rankings could shift if it is properly assessed.
- Status-quo extinction projections (EP extirpation <60 yr; WP −96% by 2040) are model projections contingent on unchanged threats, not observed outcomes.
