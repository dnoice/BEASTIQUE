# **Global Biogeography, Spatial Ecology, and Conservation Geodata of Leatherback Sea Turtles (*Dermochelys coriacea*)**

## **Evolutionary Context and Ecomorphology of the Leatherback**

The leatherback sea turtle (*Dermochelys coriacea*) stands as a profound evolutionary anomaly within the marine realm and represents the sole extant survivor of the family Dermochelyidae, a lineage that has persisted in its current form since the age of the dinosaurs.1 The species is distinguished most prominently by its highly derived carapace. Unlike the Cheloniidae family, which possesses a hard, keratinous shell, the leatherback is characterized by a thick, flexible, and rubbery dermis supported by a subdermal matrix of thousands of small, interlocking osteoderms.1 This unique morphological architecture features seven distinct longitudinal ridges running along the carapace, which optimize hydrodynamic efficiency during trans-oceanic migrations.1 This flexible exterior is critical for the species’ survival in the bathypelagic zone; it allows the turtle's body to compress under immense hydrostatic pressure, enabling the leatherback to execute dives that frequently exceed 1,000 meters in depth.2 The deepest recorded dive for the species reached an astonishing 1,344 meters (4,409 feet), surpassing the maximum dive depths of many marine mammals.1  
The geographic distribution of *Dermochelys coriacea* is the most extensive of any extant reptile on Earth.1 While nesting is fundamentally restricted to tropical and subtropical sandy beaches, the foraging range of adult leatherbacks extends deep into sub-polar and temperate latitudes.1 This vast spatial niche is unlocked by the leatherback's highly specialized physiological capacity for retaining metabolically produced heat—a phenomenon known as gigantothermy.4 Endowed with a low surface-area-to-volume ratio, extensive peripheral adipose tissue, and counter-current heat exchangers in their flippers, adult leatherbacks can maintain core body temperatures significantly higher than the ambient water, allowing them to continuously forage in frigid waters that are thermally lethal to other marine turtles.3 Consequently, geospatial mapping of telemetry data reveals a truly circumglobal presence, constrained only by the absolute freezing limits of the Arctic and Antarctic polar ice boundaries.3  
Despite this extraordinary evolutionary resilience, the global population of *Dermochelys coriacea* is undergoing a severe and rapid contraction.1 It is estimated that the global population has declined by 40 percent over the past three generations.1 Historically, the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) classified the global species as Critically Endangered in 2000; however, based on the discovery and assessment of robust Atlantic populations, the global status was upgraded to Vulnerable in 2013\.5 Yet, this aggregated global classification masks catastrophic, localized demographic collapses.5 Resolving the multifaceted anthropogenic threats to such a widely distributed marine megafauna requires high-resolution spatial definitions of both the hazardscapes and the discrete population segments they affect.6 Consequently, modern conservation biogeography heavily relies on the synthesis of disparate geospatial datasets to untangle the spatial ecology of leatherbacks and formulate targeted, dynamic management interventions.6

## **The Geospatial Infrastructure of Marine Conservation**

To monitor and manage a species with a pluriannual, trans-oceanic life cycle, the scientific community has developed highly sophisticated geodata aggregation platforms. The empirical foundation of this effort rests heavily on the State of the World's Sea Turtles (SWOT) database.8 The SWOT database aggregates sea turtle nesting and telemetry data contributed by a global network of over 550 individual researchers and projects.10 Transitioning from legacy datasets like the WIDECAST nesting database, the SWOT initiative enforces rigorous Minimum Data Standards (MDS).10 These standards establish standardized monitoring effort schemes, a modeling software program that generates total seasonal abundance estimates from partial track counts, and a classification system to label individual data records based on monitoring quality.11 This ensures that longitudinal nesting abundance and trend estimates are statistically robust and comparable across regional and global scales.10  
The SWOT database is hosted and maintained in partnership with Duke University's Marine Geospatial Ecology Lab (MGEL) via the OBIS-SEAMAP platform.12 OBIS-SEAMAP aggregates point shapefiles for nesting sites alongside vast polyline and polygon datasets derived from satellite telemetry.10 By converting raw spatial coordinates into functional ecological models, OBIS-SEAMAP allows spatial ecologists to construct predictive habitat suitability layers and network node graphs for migrating sea turtles.12 For instance, specific telemetry datasets archived in OBIS-SEAMAP, such as the ProDelphinus Peru Leatherback Tracking Project (OBIS-SEAMAP IDs 1334 and 1844), contain thousands of discrete spatial records documenting tens of thousands of kilometers traveled by tracked individuals.15 This platform is instrumental in identifying "Important Turtle Areas" at regional scales and serves as the primary data repository for subsequent global population modeling.17

### **The Regional Management Unit (RMU 2.0) Framework**

Delineating actionable spatial boundaries for highly migratory marine megafauna presents a formidable conservation challenge.7 To establish a coherent assessment structure that transcends arbitrary geopolitical borders, the IUCN Marine Turtle Specialist Group (MTSG) pioneered the concept of Regional Management Units (RMUs).6 First introduced in 2010 and substantially revised in 2023 under the moniker "RMU 2.0", this framework organizes conspecific marine turtle assemblages into distinct units above the level of individual nesting rookeries or genetic stocks, but directly below the global species level.6 RMUs are defined as spatially explicit assemblages of turtles that share critical geographic areas for breeding, foraging, and juvenile development.19 Because these individuals occupy overlapping geographic distributions, they are exposed to similar environmental drivers and anthropogenic threats, placing them on shared demographic and potentially distinct evolutionary trajectories.19  
The RMU 2.0 framework utilized advanced participatory GIS modeling to spatially integrate over 1,200 distinct datasets encompassing population genetics, tag returns, satellite telemetry, and long-term nesting beach surveys.7 Point shapefiles with precise latitude and longitude coordinates were generated for individual nesting sites.21 These were combined with georeferenced spatial layers for maternally inherited mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and biparentally inherited nuclear DNA (nDNA).21 Finally, these fine-scale levels were enveloped within the known core geographic distributions of pelagic adults derived from satellite telemetry.21 This nested envelope modeling successfully delineated a total of 48 RMUs for marine turtles globally, including 166 distinct genetic stocks.7  
For *Dermochelys coriacea*, the framework identified seven distinct RMUs, which are functionally equivalent to IUCN subpopulations: the Northwest Atlantic, Southeast Atlantic, Southwest Atlantic, Northeast Indian, Southwest Indian, East Pacific, and West Pacific.19 The delineation of these seven units provides a vital demographic baseline against which localized extinction risks can be accurately measured and mitigated.5

## **The Pacific Ocean RMUs: A Basin in Demographic Collapse**

The most acute and immediate conservation crises for leatherback turtles are currently unfolding within the Pacific Ocean basin.1 The Pacific harbors two distinct subpopulation units—the East Pacific RMU and the West Pacific RMU—both of which have suffered catastrophic collapses and are currently classified as Critically Endangered by the IUCN.5 Despite substantial multilateral conservation agreements, the precipitous decline of Pacific leatherbacks places them at an exceptionally high risk of localized extinction.1

### **The East Pacific Subpopulation**

The East Pacific RMU is defined by an astonishing 97.4% decline in nesting abundance over the past three generations.5 This subpopulation nests along the tropical and subtropical Pacific coast of the Americas, spanning an enormous geographical range from Mexico down to Ecuador.5 Standardized, systematic abundance monitoring has been executed across this region since the 1995 nesting season, revealing a highly fragmented nesting landscape consisting of 15 major nesting sites (defined as hosting \>20 nests or \>10 nests/km per year) and 149 minor, sporadic nesting sites (hosting \<20 nests per year).25  
The largest of these historical epicenters were located in Mexico and at Playa Grande in Costa Rica.25 During the late 20th century, these index beaches routinely hosted thousands of nesting females; however, rampant historical exploitation of eggs, hunting of adult females, and massive bycatch mortality have decimated the population.5 Recent demographic models estimate that the entire East Pacific population currently consists of a mere 1,007 nesting females.28 The underlying causes of this decline—particularly low hatching success and unrelenting fisheries bycatch—have not ceased and remain largely irreversible without aggressive intervention.5  
Geospatial telemetry and dive data have deeply illuminated the migratory behavior and hazard exposure of the East Pacific RMU. Upon departing Mesoamerican nesting beaches between February and April, post-nesting females traverse a distinct migratory corridor into the southeastern Pacific Ocean (SEP).29 Satellite tracking studies reveal that these females primarily migrate southward, crossing into the Southern Hemisphere to enter the South Pacific Gyre.26 Here, they utilize expansive pelagic foraging grounds in the high seas off the coasts of Peru and Chile.26 State-space modeling applied to these telemetry tracks can infer specific behaviors based on speed and turning angles; rapid, directional movements signify transiting, while highly convoluted tracks indicate Area-Restricted Search (ARS) behavior, corresponding to active foraging in nutrient-rich oceanic fronts.32  
The integration of three-dimensional tag data has revolutionized the risk assessment for this population. Dive tags identifying distinct behavioral states—such as transiting, residential/mixed-depth diving, and residential/deep diving (often reaching depths of 1,200 meters)—have been utilized to generate multidimensional risk maps.34 When these vertical habitat models are overlaid with spatial fishing effort data specific to gear type (e.g., pelagic longlines versus coastal gillnets), researchers can precisely identify the overlap between leatherback behavior and fisheries bycatch.15 High interactions with artisanal gillnet fisheries in Peru have been recognized as a primary mortality sink, prompting localized tracking initiatives to inform spatio-temporal management within the Inter-American Tropical Tuna Commission (IATTC) framework.15 Acknowledging the dire trajectory of this population, ex-situ conservation plans, colloquially termed "Plan B for Baula", are being formulated by organizations like the Center for Species Survival Argentina to create emergency protocols for the persistence of the East Pacific lineage.35

### **The West Pacific Subpopulation**

The West Pacific RMU exhibits an equally dire trajectory, having suffered an 83% decline over the past three generations.5 Historically, this subpopulation was characterized by massive nesting aggregations in Southeast Asia; however, the population in Terengganu, Malaysia, which once hosted spectacular numbers of nesting females, is now considered functionally extinct following a total collapse over the past few decades.5 Today, the remnants of the West Pacific nesting population are concentrated in the Indo-Pacific region, primarily in Indonesia (specifically Papua Barat), Papua New Guinea, and the Solomon Islands, with minor nesting events occurring in Vanuatu.5  
The geospatial and demographic dynamics of the West Pacific RMU are profoundly shaped by a bimodal seasonal nesting pattern.31 The vast majority of current nesting activity—estimated at 50% to 75% of the total subpopulation—is restricted to just two index beaches located on the Bird's Head Seascape (BHS) along the north coast of West Papua, Indonesia: the 18-kilometer beach of Jeen Yessa (formerly Jamursba Medi) and the 6-kilometer beach of Jeen Syuab (formerly Wermon).28 Nesting at Jeen Yessa peaks during the boreal summer, whereas Jeen Syuab experiences peak nesting during the boreal winter.39 To understand whether these distinct temporal patterns represent demographically isolated populations, geneticists compared 763-bp sequences of the mtDNA control region alongside data from 17 microsatellite loci, employing reduced-representation (RAD-capture) techniques.40 While the analysis detected overall low genetic diversity, it identified 11 mtDNA haplotypes and confirmed a shared genetic lineage that nonetheless executes highly divergent spatial dispersal.40 Recent demographic analyses project a mean of only 318 females nesting annually across these critical index sites, with long-term data (2001-2017) indicating an annual decline of nearly 6%, despite a slight stabilization in recent years due to improved nest protection.28  
The post-nesting migrations of the West Pacific RMU are among the longest and most complex of any vertebrate. Between 2000 and 2007, an extensive telemetry project deployed 126 satellite tags on West Pacific leatherbacks, definitively linking the temporal nesting seasons to specific, distant foraging grounds.43 Winter-nesting females departing from Papua New Guinea, Indonesia, and the Solomon Islands migrate southward into the temperate waters of the South Pacific, utilizing foraging grounds in the Tasman Front, the East Australian Current Extension, and waters off New Zealand.31 Conversely, summer-nesting females execute a massive trans-Pacific migration.43 Traversing the central North Pacific, these individuals migrate to the California Current Ecosystem off the West Coast of the United States to forage on abundant aggregations of brown sea nettles (jellyfish).31  
The core pelagic habitat within the California Current is characterized by low sea surface temperatures and specific chlorophyll-a signatures.37 However, this highly productive zone is heavily utilized by commercial fisheries. While the establishment of the Pacific Leatherback Conservation Area off California and Oregon in 2001 successfully implemented seasonal drift gillnet closures, the population continues to hemorrhage.44 Aerial surveys conducted off the West Coast since 1990—originally intended to count harbor porpoises—reveal that the foraging population of leatherbacks has declined by 5.6% per year, culminating in an 80% total drop.44 Alarmingly, environmental modeling predicts that the core pelagic habitat for Pacific leatherbacks will shrink by an additional 15% within the next century due to climate-driven oceanographic alterations, compounding the extinction risk for the summer-nesting cohort.37

## **The Atlantic Ocean RMUs: A Tapestry of Growth, Stability, and Deep Vulnerability**

In stark contrast to the uniform collapse in the Pacific, the Atlantic Ocean presents a highly complex biogeographical landscape for *Dermochelys coriacea*. The basin hosts three distinct RMUs—the Northwest Atlantic, the Southeast Atlantic, and the Southwest Atlantic—that span the full spectrum of IUCN threat categories, from localized exponential growth to data-deficient ambiguity.

### **The Northwest Atlantic Subpopulation**

The Northwest Atlantic (NWA) RMU has historically been considered one of the two largest and most robust leatherback subpopulations in the world.5 Its expansive nesting range encompasses the Wider Caribbean Region, the Guiana Shield (including highly productive beaches in Suriname and French Guiana), Trinidad and Tobago, and the southeastern United States, with a concentration along the Atlantic coast of Florida.5 Based on an estimated 20.6% increase in abundance over the previous three generations and a massive reproductive output exceeding 50,000 nests annually, the IUCN assessed this RMU as Least Concern in 2013\.5 High-fidelity index sites provided the empirical backbone for this optimistic assessment. For example, at the Sandy Point National Wildlife Refuge on St. Croix in the U.S. Virgin Islands (geographically located at 17°40′40″N 64°54′0″W), meticulous flipper tagging and monitoring since 1977 recorded annual population growth rates of approximately 13% through the 1990s and early 2000s.46 Similarly, standardized statewide surveys in Florida documented an exponential rise from 98 nests in 1989 to nearly 1,000 nests annually two decades later.48  
However, recent geospatial data and abundance monitoring have revealed a sharp and concerning reversal of these positive trends. Significant decreases in nesting activity have been documented across numerous key locations, including the formerly booming Florida index beaches, prompting the IUCN to downgrade the NWA RMU status to Endangered in 2019\.1 The deteriorating status of the population triggered legal and regulatory scrutiny. In 2017, the Blue Water Fishermen's Association petitioned the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) and the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) to identify the NWA population as a Distinct Population Segment (DPS) under the Endangered Species Act (ESA) and subsequently downlist it to Threatened.49 Following an exhaustive 12-month status review utilizing the policy regarding the Recognition of Distinct Vertebrate Population Segments, the agencies concluded in August 2020 that isolating the NWA DPS was not warranted.50 Consequently, the NWA leatherback retains its global Endangered listing under the ESA, maintaining stringent federal protections across U.S. jurisdictions.1  
Spatial tracking of the NWA RMU has illuminated a highly complex, size-dependent migratory corridor extending deep into northern latitudes. Adult leatherbacks commonly migrate from nesting beaches in the Caribbean or foraging grounds in the South and Mid-Atlantic Bights northward to highly productive summer foraging arenas off New England, Nova Scotia, and the Gulf of St. Lawrence in Canada.1 Telemetry studies reveal a distinct spatial segregation based on body size and thermoregulatory capacity.4 Large adult turtles, aided by their massive lipid stores and low surface-area-to-volume ratio, can effectively retain metabolically produced heat, allowing them to exploit the frigid, prey-dense waters of the Canadian shelf.4 Conversely, smaller sub-adult leatherbacks, lacking sufficient thermal inertia, are largely restricted to the relatively warmer waters of the Northeast US shelf.4  
These extensive migratory corridors traverse heavily industrialized coastal and pelagic zones, exposing the NWA RMU to diverse anthropogenic hazards. Georeferenced stranding and necropsy data routinely attribute mortality to entanglement in pot gear, trap nets, and heavy fishing lines, resulting in drowning, ligature wounds, and asphyxiation.54 Furthermore, marine pollution poses a critical threat; necropsies of stranded leatherbacks in the Northwest Atlantic frequently reveal severe gastrointestinal blockages caused by the ingestion of soft plastics, plastic sheeting, and bags, which the turtles fatally mistake for gelatinous prey.55 This threat is recognized internationally, with the OSPAR Commission adopting "litter ingested by sea turtles" as a common marine litter indicator to spur transboundary mitigation.54

### **The Southeast Atlantic Subpopulation**

The Southeast Atlantic (SEA) RMU represents a profound biogeographical paradox: it is potentially the largest leatherback subpopulation on Earth, yet it remains classified globally as Data Deficient by the IUCN.5 The geographic epicenter of this RMU is situated along the coast of central Africa, with significant nesting in the Republic of Congo and Bioko (Equatorial Guinea), and minor nesting extending north to Senegal and south to Angola.5 However, the absolute stronghold of the SEA RMU is the 372-mile coastline of Gabon.5  
A landmark six-year study conducted by the University of Exeter and the Wildlife Conservation Society revealed that Gabon hosts the world's largest nesting population of leatherback turtles, estimating a population of 16,000 to 41,000 nesting females.57 Within Gabon, Pongara National Park serves as a critical nesting hub; along its 35 km Atlantic coastline, approximately 3,500 nests are laid annually between October and March.58 The preservation of this massive RMU is highly correlated with aggressive domestic conservation policies, as roughly 80% of the species' nesting habitat in Gabon lies within an extensive network of national parks and protected areas.57 Despite this localized abundance, the lack of continuous, decades-long monitoring datasets precludes the calculation of a definitive generational trend, resulting in the Data Deficient listing.5  
The pelagic spatial ecology of the SEA RMU has been dramatically expanded through extensive satellite tracking initiatives. Pan-Atlantic telemetry studies utilizing state-of-the-art MK10-A and MK10-AF Platform Transmitter Terminals (PTTs) tracked the post-nesting movements of 106 adult females from Gabon between 1995 and 2010\.59 Geospatial analysis of these tracks categorized their behavior into three distinct dispersal strategies: migrating westward to equatorial Atlantic habitats, traversing southwest toward temperate habitats off South America, or moving directly south toward temperate habitats off southern Africa.59 These transatlantic tracks definitively shattered the paradigm of ocean-basin isolation, linking the coastal rookeries of central Africa directly to the foraging waters of Argentina and Brazil.61 While executing these strategies, SEA leatherbacks actively seek regions with high surface chlorophyll concentrations.59 Unfortunately, spatial mapping reveals that these critical foraging zones intersect directly with some of the world's most concentrated pelagic longline fishing efforts, placing the turtles at extreme risk on the high seas where the implementation of legal conventions is exceedingly complex.59

### **The Southwest Atlantic Subpopulation**

At the southern extreme of the species' Western Atlantic range lies the Southwest Atlantic (SWA) RMU. This subpopulation represents a fascinating case study in micro-population dynamics. It is classified as Critically Endangered, harboring a diminutive population of fewer than 50 mature individuals (with some estimates placing the number around 35).5 Despite its perilous absolute size, the SWA RMU exhibits an extraordinary population trajectory, boasting a 232% growth in nesting abundance over the past three generations.5  
Nesting is almost exclusively restricted to a highly localized 160-kilometer stretch of coastline in the state of Espírito Santo, southeastern Brazil, centered around the Reserva Biológica de Comboios (with index beaches extending across locations such as Povoação, Guriri, and Itaúnas).62 Between 1988 and 2017, the mean number of annual nests at this site increased from 25.6 in the first five years of monitoring to 89.8 in the final five years.63 Concurrently, spatial ecologists recorded a significant decrease in the mean curved carapace length of the nesting population.63 This morphological shift strongly indicates a successful recruitment of young, newly mature females into the breeding population, a direct demographic dividend of strict nest protection and daily dawn patrols initiated by Projeto TAMAR-ICMBio in the 1980s.63  
Despite these positive demographic indicators, the SWA RMU remains highly vulnerable. Genetic profiling demonstrates that the Espírito Santo rookery constitutes a unique RMU, genetically distinct from other Atlantic populations.66 Georeferenced tracking of post-nesting females from Comboios reveals localized internesting and post-nesting movements into adjacent coastal and oceanic foraging areas off Uruguay and the northern coast of Argentina.62 Recent parasitological investigations along the Argentine coast have further enriched the biogeographical understanding of this RMU.69 Researchers utilizing morphological and molecular tools identified novel host-parasite associations, such as the digeneans *Pyelosomum renicapite* and *Orchidasma amphiorchis*, which serve as biological tags to infer finer-scale spatial connectivity and ontogenetic shifts within the RMU.69  
A looming, systemic threat to the SWA RMU is climate change. Because sex determination in sea turtles is temperature-dependent, thermal variations in the nesting sand dictate the sex ratio of the hatchlings. Research utilizing incubation durations at Espírito Santo as a proxy for sand temperature suggests that the thermal characteristics of the beach are increasingly conducive to female-biased sex ratios.66 As global temperatures rise, these highly restricted, male-producing temperate beaches may cross critical thermal thresholds, threatening the future demographic stability and reproductive viability of this recovering subpopulation.66

## **The Indian Ocean RMUs: Remoteness, Oceanography, and Enduring Monitoring**

The Indian Ocean hosts two distinct leatherback RMUs that represent opposing extremes of geographic isolation and monitoring history, illustrating the diverse challenges of global sea turtle conservation.

### **The Northeast Indian Subpopulation**

The Northeast Indian (NEI) RMU is classified globally as Data Deficient.5 This designation stems primarily from the extreme geographic remoteness of its nesting grounds, which has historically hindered the collection of comprehensive genetic data, threat assessments, and continuous long-term abundance datasets.5 The most significant nesting aggregations for this subpopulation occur in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands of India, with lesser contributions from Sri Lanka (e.g., Godavaya and Rekawa beaches) and Sumatra, Indonesia.5  
Within the Andaman and Nicobar archipelago, the RMU relies on foundational rookeries located on West Bay and South Bay of Little Andaman Island, and critically, Galathea Bay on Great Nicobar Island.73 Galathea Bay is a site of immense biological significance and conservation contention. Despite historical setbacks, the bay currently hosts hundreds of leatherback nests annually (e.g., 649 nests in 2022, 505 in 2023, and 617 in 2024).74 The ecological complexity of this site is heightened by its overlapping use as a crucial nesting ground for the endemic Nicobar megapode, an intersection of biodiversity that complicates large-scale coastal development proposals.74 Overall, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands support over 1,000 leatherback nests per season, and longitudinal surveys by organizations like the Dakshin Foundation indicate that this specific nesting population remains demographically stable, exhibiting resilience to historical disturbances like the 2004 tsunami.71  
Recent advancements in satellite telemetry and physical oceanography have begun to untangle the cryptic post-nesting spatial ecology of the NEI RMU. Tracking studies of leatherbacks departing from Little Andaman Island reveal extensive migrations across the Bay of Bengal and the broader Indian Ocean.76 Geospatial analysis of these tracks highlights the profound influence of mesoscale oceanographic features on marine turtle navigation. Telemetry data shows that west-traveling turtles frequently interact with oceanic eddies; individuals routinely loop around the edges of these spinning water masses or swim tangentially between them.78 In some instances, tagged turtles adopted a 'resident' behavioral state, remaining entrapped within specific eddy systems for over a month.78  
These complex migratory tracks ultimately terminate in areas exhibiting high chlorophyll concentrations and primary productivity, confirming that NEI leatherbacks precisely target dense biological hotspots.76 Furthermore, to understand the early life-history stages of this RMU, researchers applied Lagrangian drift models to simulate the passive dispersal of hatchlings departing the Andaman Islands.76 The models suggest that neonates remain entrained within the Bay of Bengal's closed current systems for 5 to 10 years.76 This implies that active swimming is an absolute physiological requisite for juveniles to eventually breach these regional currents and reach the adult pelagic feeding grounds across the wider ocean basin.76

### **The Southwest Indian Subpopulation**

In sharp contrast to the data-poor northern basin, the Southwest Indian (SWI) RMU benefits from one of the longest continuous marine turtle monitoring programs in existence.5 Over 50 years of standardized nesting data have been aggregated for this population, which is concentrated almost entirely along the Indian Ocean coast of South Africa's KwaZulu-Natal province, specifically within the strictly protected iSimangaliso Wetland Park.5 Minor nesting also occurs further north in Mozambique.5  
Despite decades of rigorous local conservation, the IUCN classifies the SWI RMU as Critically Endangered.5 The subpopulation is extremely small—containing approximately 148 mature individuals—and continues to exhibit a slow demographic decay, with a 5.6% decline recorded over the past three generations.5 The marine habitat utilized by the SWI RMU is geographically complex, extending southward from the nesting beaches and wrapping around the Cape of Good Hope, thereby spanning the turbulent oceanographic boundary between the Indian and Atlantic Oceans.5 The strict spatial confinement of its nesting range along the South African coast, coupled with high bycatch mortality in the surrounding international pelagic fisheries, leaves this diminutive population highly susceptible to both localized stochastic events and broad-scale oceanic industrialization.

## **Conclusion: Synthesizing Geodata for Dynamic Ocean Management**

The aggregation, standardization, and analysis of global geospatial data—ranging from high-resolution satellite telemetry and participatory GIS mapping to genomic sequencing and parasitological tagging—has revolutionized the conservation biogeography of *Dermochelys coriacea*. The IUCN Regional Management Unit framework (RMU 2.0) has successfully stratified a seemingly homogenous, circumglobal species into seven distinct demographic and evolutionary trajectories. This spatial stratification reveals a stark and urgent reality: while intensive, localized beach-level conservation has fostered remarkable recoveries (as seen in the Southwest Atlantic) and maintained stability (as in the Northeast Indian RMU), these terrestrial gains are rapidly negated by the spatial overlap of leatherback migratory corridors with industrialized pelagic fisheries.  
The bimodal migrations of the West Pacific RMU to the California Current, the trans-Atlantic voyages of the Southeast Atlantic RMU, and the deep-diving gyre interactions of the East Pacific RMU all underscore a fundamental spatial mismatch in modern marine conservation. The geopolitical boundaries and exclusive economic zones that dictate marine policy rarely align with the dynamic oceanographic features—such as eddies, thermal fronts, and chlorophyll concentrations—that dictate leatherback spatial ecology. Moving forward, the preservation of the leatherback sea turtle relies not only on maintaining rigorous minimum data standards for nesting abundances but on the operationalization of multidimensional geodata. By integrating three-dimensional dive profiles with real-time oceanographic modeling and satellite tracking of commercial fishing vessels, spatial ecologists and policymakers can pioneer dynamic ocean management. Establishing predictive, mobile marine protected areas that shift in real-time with oceanographic fronts represents the only viable strategy to safeguard this ancient mariner across the high seas, mitigating bycatch and ensuring the survival of the species across all seven of its global domains.
