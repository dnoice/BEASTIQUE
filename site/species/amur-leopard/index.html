Species Profile

Siberia's Rarest Predator

The Amur leopard (Panthera pardus orientalis) is the world's rarest big cat and one of the most endangered animals on Earth. Named after the Amur River that marks the border between Russia and China, this solitary predator roams the temperate forests of Primorsky Krai in the Russian Far East.

Unlike its African and Indian cousins, the Amur leopard has adapted to life in one of the harshest climates any big cat endures. It grows a thick, pale coat in winter — up to 7 centimeters long — that lightens to blend with snow-covered landscapes. Its legs are proportionally longer than other leopard subspecies, an adaptation for wading through deep snow.

Each Amur leopard carries a unique pattern of rosettes — irregular dark rings with lighter centers — that serves as a natural fingerprint. Researchers use these patterns to identify and track individuals in camera trap surveys across the leopard's remaining range.

Remarkable Facts

Predator Unveiled

Fact 01

Ghost of the Forest

The Amur leopard is one of the most elusive predators on the planet. Strictly nocturnal and fiercely solitary, it patrols territories of up to 120 square miles through dense taiga forest. Camera traps remain the primary means of studying these phantoms — some individuals go years without a single confirmed sighting by human eyes.

Fact 02

Olympic Athlete

Built for explosive power, the Amur leopard can leap up to 19 feet horizontally and 10 feet vertically from a standing position. At full sprint, it reaches 37 miles per hour — fast enough to chase down a deer in open terrain. This combination of agility and raw strength makes it one of the most formidable ambush predators in the wild.

Fact 03

Unique Identity

Every Amur leopard wears a coat as distinctive as a human fingerprint. The arrangement of rosettes — irregular dark rings encircling lighter patches — is unique to each individual. Conservation scientists photograph both flanks of every leopard encountered, building visual databases that allow them to identify, track, and monitor the health of the entire population.

Fact 04

Winter Warrior

Temperatures in the Amur leopard's homeland plunge to -40°F in winter. To survive, it grows a dense winter coat up to 7 centimeters long — more than double its summer fur. This coat shifts from a rich reddish-yellow in summer to a pale, creamy gold in winter, providing seasonal camouflage against snow-dusted forests and frozen riverbeds.

Fact 05

Tree Vault

After making a kill, the Amur leopard drags its prey — often two to three times its own body weight — high into the branches of a tree. This extraordinary feat of strength protects the meal from scavengers like bears and tigers. A single deer carcass cached in the canopy can sustain a leopard for over a week.

Fact 06

Living Relic

The Amur leopard's lineage stretches back to the Pleistocene epoch, when great ice sheets sculpted the land it still calls home. While saber-toothed cats, cave lions, and woolly mammoths vanished, this leopard subspecies endured through sheer adaptability. It is the northernmost leopard population on Earth and the only one evolved to thrive in boreal snowscapes.

Critically Endangered

On the Edge of Forever

With fewer than 120 individuals left in the wild, the Amur leopard holds the grim distinction of being the world's rarest big cat. Its entire population is confined to a narrow strip of temperate forest in Russia's Primorsky Krai and the borderlands of northeastern China — a range smaller than most national parks. One disease outbreak, one severe fire season, or one infrastructure project could erase what remains.

The establishment of Land of the Leopard National Park in 2012 marked a turning point, uniting fragmented habitat under formal protection. Camera-trap surveys show numbers have slowly climbed from a low of roughly 30 in the early 2000s, but the species remains critically endangered, and long-term survival depends on corridor connectivity, anti-poaching enforcement, and genetic rescue programs.
