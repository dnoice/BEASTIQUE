Species Profile

The Last of an Ancient Line

The leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea) is the largest sea turtle on Earth and one of the most extraordinary reptiles alive. Where every other sea turtle carries a hard, bony shell, the leatherback is sheathed in tough, rubbery skin raised into seven long ridges — a carapace built for the open ocean rather than the reef.

It is a creature of superlatives: the deepest-diving reptile, plunging beyond 1,200 metres into the lightless deep; a near-warm-blooded titan that holds its body heat in seas cold enough to kill other turtles; and the most widely distributed reptile on the planet, ranging from the tropics to the edge of the Arctic in pursuit of a single, gelatinous prey — jellyfish.

Its lineage is older than almost anything else swimming today, reaching back more than a hundred million years to seas that mosasaurs once ruled. Yet this deep-time survivor now faces a thoroughly modern gauntlet — plastic, longlines, poached nests, and a warming climate quietly rewriting the next generation.

Remarkable Facts

Titan of Deep Time

Fact 01

The Shell That Isn't

Alone among sea turtles, the leatherback has no hard shell. Its back is a flexible mosaic of thousands of tiny bones embedded in tough, oil-soaked skin, raised into seven long ridges that channel water as it swims. The name says it all — Dermochelys means "skin turtle," a hull of leather built for the open ocean.

Fact 02

Into the Crushing Deep

It is the deepest-diving reptile alive, recorded at 1,280 metres — deeper than most whales. Its flexible shell and collapsible lungs let it shrug off pressure that would crush a hard-shelled turtle, while its heartbeat slows to a crawl. The leatherback hunts in the twilight and midnight zones where few air-breathers dare to follow.

Fact 03

Warm Heart in Cold Seas

Through "gigantothermy," the leatherback keeps its body up to 18°C warmer than the water around it. Sheer bulk, an insulating layer of fat, and countercurrent heat exchange in its flippers let it forage in seas off Norway, Newfoundland, and Chile — water cold enough to kill any other sea turtle outright.

Fact 04

Built to Swallow the Sea

The leatherback is a jellyfish specialist, and its throat is a one-way trap: hundreds of backward-pointing keratin spines line the mouth and esophagus so gelatinous prey can never slip free. It can eat its own body weight in jellyfish in a day — which is exactly why a single drifting plastic bag, mistaken for a jelly, can be fatal.

Fact 05

The Ocean Wanderer

No reptile roams farther. Leatherbacks undertake transoceanic migrations of up to 16,000 kilometres between tropical nesting beaches and distant feeding grounds, navigating the open blue with uncanny precision. A turtle that hatches in Indonesia may feed off the coast of California — a lifetime spent crossing entire oceans.

Fact 06

Survivor of Deep Time

The leatherback is the sole survivor of an entire family, Dermochelyidae, whose roots reach back more than 100 million years to the Cretaceous. It shared the seas with mosasaurs and outlasted the asteroid that ended the dinosaurs. Its body plan has barely changed since — a design the ocean has not improved upon in a hundred million years.

Vulnerable

A Wanderer Running Out of Ocean

Globally listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List, the leatherback's story differs sharply from sea to sea. Atlantic populations are holding on, but the Pacific subpopulations have collapsed by more than 90% and are now critically endangered. Decades of bycatch, plundered nests, and an ocean filling with plastic have hollowed out a species that survived the death of the dinosaurs.

The fixes are known and they work where they are applied: protected nesting beaches, turtle-excluder devices and circle hooks that cut bycatch, and international agreements like CITES and the Convention on Migratory Species. The hard part is scale — a turtle that crosses whole ocean basins can only be saved by cleaner seas and safer fisheries across all of them at once.
